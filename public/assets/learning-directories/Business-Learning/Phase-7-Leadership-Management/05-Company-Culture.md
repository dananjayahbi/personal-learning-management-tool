### The Stages of Culture

**Stage 1: Founders (1-5 people)**
```
Culture = Founders' behavior
- Every action sets precedent
- Easy to course-correct
- Everyone knows everyone
- Informal norms
- Critical foundation period

Your job: Model what you want to see
```

**Stage 2: Early Team (5-20 people)**
```
Culture = Founders + First hires
- First hires amplify or dilute culture
- Need to articulate what was implicit
- Start documenting norms
- Hire for culture fit becomes critical

Your job: Hire cultural ambassadors
```

**Stage 3: Scaling (20-50 people)**
```
Culture = Systems and reinforcement
- Can't rely on osmosis anymore
- Need explicit rituals and processes
- Some founding team members may not scale
- Subcultures emerge

Your job: Systematize culture
```

**Stage 4: Mature (50+ people)**
```
Culture = Self-perpetuating system
- New employees trained by existing
- Culture deck and onboarding
- Regular culture surveys
- Dedicated people ops

Your job: Maintain and evolve
```

## Hiring for Culture Fit

### Culture Fit vs Culture Add

**Culture Fit** (Traditional):
"Do they match our current culture?"
Risk: Homogeneity, groupthink, lack of diversity

**Culture Add** (Better):
"Do they share our values AND bring new perspectives?"
Result: Values alignment + diversity of thought

**The Balance**:
```
Must Align (Non-negotiable):
- Core values
- Work ethic
- Integrity
- Communication style (high-level)

Should Differ (Desirable):
- Background and experiences
- Perspectives and approaches
- Skills and expertise
- Personality styles
```

## Scaling Culture

### The First Culture Crisis (Around 20-30 people)

**What Happens**:
```
- Can't onboard everyone personally
- New people don't "get it" automatically
- Subcultures form
- Original team feels culture is diluting
- Founders can't be everywhere
- What was implicit must become explicit
```

**How to Navigate**:

**1. Document Everything**:
```
Create:
- Culture deck
- Values definitions with examples
- "How we work" handbook
- Decision-making frameworks
- Meeting norms
- Communication guidelines
- Onboarding checklist
```

**2. Designate Culture Carriers**:
```
Identify 3-5 people who embody culture:
- Involve in all hiring
- Lead onboarding
- Amplify cultural messages
- Call out violations
- Model behaviors

Don't call them "Culture Police" (negative)
Call them "Culture Champions" (positive)
```

**3. Systematize Recognition**:
```
Move from:
- Ad hoc shout-outs
To:
- Structured recognition program
- Values-based awards
- Regular celebration rituals
- Public acknowledgment system
```

**4. Create Feedback Loops**:
```
Monthly Culture Survey (5 questions):
1. Do you understand our values? (1-10)
2. Do you see values in action daily? (1-10)
3. Do you feel heard and valued? (1-10)
4. Would you recommend working here? (1-10)
5. What would improve our culture?

Track trends, address issues quickly
```

### Maintaining Culture Through Growth

**50+ People**:
```
Challenges:
- Multiple teams/departments
- Layers of management
- Remote team members
- Different locations
- Acquired companies/teams

Solutions:
- Dedicated People Ops person
- Regular all-hands (don't skip)
- Cross-functional projects

- Culture ambassadors in each team
```

```
At this scale:

- Subcultures are acknowledged and managed
- You hire executives who protect culture
```

## Common Culture Killers

### 1. Tolerating Brilliant Jerks

**The Trap**:
```
"Yes, they're difficult, but they're so talented/productive!"

Impact:
- Good people leave
- Culture erodes
- Team becomes toxic
- Collaboration breaks down
- Innovation decreases
```

**The Solution**:
```
Netflix's approach: "Adequate performance + Great culture fit" 
beats "Brilliant performance + Culture destroyer"

Fire the brilliant jerk. Every time. Quickly.

The team will celebrate (internally or openly).
```

**How to Handle**:
```
1. Have direct conversation
   "Your work is excellent, but your behavior is damaging the team. 
   Here are specific examples. This must change."

2. Give clear expectations and timeline
   "In the next 30 days, I need to see [specific behaviors]"

3. Follow up weekly

4. If no change, let them go
   "Despite your technical skills, your behavior doesn't align 
   with our values. Today is your last day."

5. Tell the team why (without trashing them)
   "We parted ways because of cultural misalignment. We value 
   collaboration and respect above all, even technical excellence."
```

### 2. Values-Actions Mismatch

**The Trap**:
```
Say: "We value work-life balance"
Do: Praise people working weekends

Say: "We value transparency"
Do: Make decisions behind closed doors

Say: "We value diversity"
Do: Hire people all from same background

Team sees: You don't mean what you say
```

**The Solution**:
```
Audit your stated values vs actual behaviors:
- What do you reward?
- What do you punish?
- How do you spend time?
- What behaviors do leaders model?

If there's mismatch:
Either change the values or change the behaviors.
```

### 3. Growing Too Fast

**The Trap**:
```
Pressure to grow quickly â†’ Lower hiring bar â†’ Wrong people â†’ 
Culture dilutes â†’ Good people leave â†’ More pressure to hire â†’ 
Even lower bar â†’ Death spiral
```

**The Solution**:
```
Grow as fast as you can hire A+ players, no faster.

Better to:
- Turn down customers
- Delay features
- Grow slower
- Miss revenue targets

Than to:
- Hire wrong people
- Destroy culture
- Lose great team members
- Build unsustainable company
```

### 4. Founder Disconnect

**The Trap**:
```
Early: Founder knows everyone, models culture daily
Later: Founder in meetings all day, team rarely sees them
Result: Culture drift, team feels disconnected
```

**The Solution**:
```
Schedule Regular:
- Lunch with random team member (weekly)
- "Office hours" (anyone can book 15 min)
- Working sessions (code/design/sales with team)
- All-hands Q&A (don't skip)
- Respond in Slack (be visible)
- Attend team events (not above it)

You set the culture. You must be present.
```

### The Exit Interview

**When Someone Leaves** (Even if fired):
```
Ask (in person or video, not email):
1. What did you like most about working here?
2. What frustrated you most?
3. Did you feel your contributions were valued?
4. How was your relationship with your manager?
5. What would have made you stay?
6. What should we change?
7. Would you work here again in the future?
8. Would you recommend us to others?

Listen without defending. Thank them for honesty.
Take notes. Look for patterns across exits.
```
